Observe

The more you observe, the less you will be confused.

📅 April 2026 📍 Parc Josaphat, Brussels ⏱ 4 min read

I came to a park straight from the office today. I did not plan to. I just needed to do something that was not a screen, and I needed to listen.

There are very salient features in nature, but you only see them if you are quiet. You have to walk and observe calmly. You have to stop looking at things and start noticing them — the way the water moves around a stone, the way the wind lifts one leaf and not another, the symmetry of a flower you have walked past a hundred times without seeing. None of it is new. All of it is new, because you have never really looked before.

What struck me, walking in the park, is how much patience nature asks of anyone who wants to understand it. A plant does a thing, an animal does a thing, the water does a thing. If you see it only once, you have seen nothing — you have seen a coincidence. You have to come back. You have to watch the same bend of the same stream on a different evening, in a different wind, and slowly a pattern emerges that was not visible in any single moment. It takes time. It is not like you observed once and you understood. You have to repeat, and repeat, and repeat.

This is, strangely, exactly how statistics works. I have spent years thinking about this, even written about it. A single observation tells you almost nothing. Two, tell you slightly more. A hundred, begin to reveal the shape of the thing underneath — the real pattern, separated from the noise. Any working scientist knows this in their bones. You do not draw an inference from one experiment. You sample. You repeat. You accumulate. And the truth emerges from the repetition, not from any single look.

And here is the thing that stopped me today, sitting quietly on that bench. The AI models I work with every day, the large language models, the pattern-finders, the systems that feel almost magical when they work — they learn in exactly the same way. They do not understand from one example. They see millions. They update, very slightly, with each one. They converge on pattern through repetition and probability, which is the same way a patient observer in a park converges on the truth of a stream.

This cannot be a coincidence. The mathematics of learning from repeated observation is the same mathematics whether the observer is a human being sitting or walking quietly, a scientist running multiple trials, or a neural network processing tokens. We did not invent it. We borrowed it — from gravity, from symmetry, from the slow accumulation of signal against noise that life itself has been doing for four billion years. Every learning system we have ever built was drawn from nature, even if we forgot to say so.

So when people ask me how to think more clearly, or how to make a better decision, or how to find a pattern in something that looks like chaos, I find myself reaching for the same small sentence again and again. Observe. The more you observe, the less you will be confused. That is what nature teaches. That is what statistics works on. That is how the AI learns. Probably that is the only way anything is ever really known.

I came to the park to rest my eyes. I am leaving with a quieter thought — that the machines we are so proud of building are perhaps just us, finally learning to listen the way the park has been listening all along. And that if I want to understand anything — a plant, a pattern, a person, the universe — I will have to do what the park does. Sit still. Come back tomorrow. And the day after. And the day after that.

Those are the things.

✦ ✦ ✦

Parc Josaphat, Brussels — April 2026
