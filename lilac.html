How Does the Lilac Know?

On plants, patience, and the strange intelligence of things without brains.

📅 April 2026 📍 Brussels ⏱ 4 min read

While in the Park over the weekend, I walked past a lilac and then I stopped. Small pink flowers, hundreds of them, all open on the same branch, at the same time, in the same week they opened last year. I have walked past this bush every April for years. And this April, for the first time, I asked the obvious question.

How does the lilac know it is April?

It has no eyes. It has no brain. It has no nervous system. It cannot read a calendar. It cannot ask a friend. And yet every spring, without fail, it decides — now, the cold is over, now is the time to open. And it is not only this lilac. The cherry trees in the long park avenue do the same thing, on roughly the same week. The maples do it a little earlier. The chestnuts do it a little later. Each tree keeps its own quiet calendar, and each one keeps it without anything we would normally call a mind.

When you sit with this for a while, it stops feeling like decoration. It starts feeling like a real question.

The honest answer is that plants do something we do not have a good word for. They sense how long the day is by chemicals in their leaves. They count cold days, so they know whether winter is really over or only pretending. They measure temperature, light, and even the colour of the light falling on them. They send signals between their roots and their leaves. They sometimes send signals to other plants, through the soil. And increasingly we are finding that they remember things — a plant that survived a drought one year behaves differently when the next drought comes, even though it has no neurons, no memory cells, nothing we would call a brain. This is not mysticism. This is ordinary, published, repeated plant biology. And yet when you describe it in human words, none of the human words fit properly.

If you call it thinking, every careful plant biologist will push back, because thinking is a word we built for brains. If you call it just chemistry, you have technically described it, but you have also missed what is strange about it — because we are also just chemistry, and nobody is satisfied with that as an explanation of human thought.

So what is the plant doing?

I think the honest answer is that the plant is doing something that looks remarkably like thinking, without any of the equipment we thought thinking required. And that should unsettle us. Not because the lilac is secretly conscious. But because it means our definition of intelligence was quietly assuming something it should not have assumed. We took it for granted that to know things, you needed a brain. The lilac in Parc Josaphat has not read that rule.

This matters beyond the curiosity of it. We are living in a moment where we are building machines that learn, decide, and behave intelligently, also without brains — different hardware, different substrate, same outcome. And we keep arguing about whether those machines really think, as if the question has an easy answer. Maybe the lilac is the older version of the same question. Maybe nature has been running experiments in non-brain intelligence for a very long time, and we are only just beginning to notice.

The next time you walk past a flowering tree this spring, try to remember — it made a decision to open today. It read the season correctly. It timed itself to the bees that have not yet arrived, but soon will. It did all of this with no eyes, no brain, and no help from us. And then ask yourself, honestly: How does the lilac know?

I do not have a clean answer. But the question feels bigger than it looked.

Let's ignite our curiosity — not only in this spring, but always.

✦ ✦ ✦

Brussels — April 2026
